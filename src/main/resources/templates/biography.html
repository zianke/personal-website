BIOGRAPHY
Zian Ke
As a senior student majoring in Computer Science at University of Michigan, I have strong skills and sufficient experience in software engineering. In my role as a full-stack developer and back-end architect in the past three years, I have led several projects including AuraMaze and CSSA Apps, in charge of front-end framework design, RESTful API implementation, search engine development, distributed database management, cloud server deployment and quality assurance.

(Following is a copy of my Personal Statement in graduate school application.)

Before I started my journey in Computer Science, there is another thing that made equally significant influence on me, Art. I began playing the piano at elementary school, and later won the Silver Award in the "Future Star" National Youth Piano Competition in China. Piano opened the door to classical music for me, and led me into the concerts of world-famous soloists, as well as conductors and orchestras. My passion for classical music was so strong that I determined to promote it to more people and let them enjoy its beauty.

This dream motivated me to launch a project named "Classical Gramophone" at Shanghai High School. Apart from holding lectures and broadcast programs on the history of music, the core of this project is to produce classical music apps for mobile device users, so that they can have access to music data more conveniently. Despite being a neophyte in programming at that time, I collaborated with professional developers in iOS and Android, and made use of a little Visual Basic knowledge learnt from class to build the Windows application. With the publication on App Store (China) and the high praise from my friends, I realized the power of computer programming for the first time, and desired to utilize coding to make more of my ideas come true. That's my initial motivation of choosing Computer Science as my undergraduate major.

My freshman and sophomore years at Shanghai Jiao Tong University largely enriched my knowledge and broadened my horizon. My role as Tech Leader in two full-stack projects, Teaching Assistant Recruitment System for UM-SJTU Joint Institute, and Online Appraisal System for Huawei Technologies, enabled me to build comprehensive technology stacks in software engineering, from front-end framework to back-end architecture, and from database management system to cloud server deployment. Besides, my first research project Fingerprint Minutiae Extraction made me fascinated by the power of Deep Learning especially CNN model for the first time. That's why I have been exploring the most cutting-edge AI technologies since then. Meanwhile, thanks to the exchange program at Technical University of Berlin in January 2017, I had a golden opportunity to travel around seven European countries, in pursuit of the precious artworks at Rijksmuseum, the extraordinary sound of Vienna Philharmonic Orchestra, and the monumental French Gothic Cathedrals in Chartres, Reims, Amiens, etc. These great achievements in art history often arouse my reflection that, as a Computer Science student, despite being at the forefront of the technology growth, together with contributing with something that no one else has ever made, we still require looking back to the brilliant human history, together with learning from those contributions that no one else could ever surpass.

I was lucky to be selected as a Dual Degree Program student, and started my journey at University of Michigan in September 2017. After taking several upper-level CS courses which further enhanced my skills in Software Systems and Artificial Intelligence, I realized that the crossroad of these two domains is Big Data, and eventually established it as my future academic and career goal. However, another question came to me: What kind of impact should Big Data have on Art, considering it's one of the most humanistic domains and should never be replaced by machine. I finally found my answer from Auguste Rodin. 2017 was the 100th anniversary of Rodin's death. While traveling across the U.S. to study his works, the book Rodin on Art and Artists enlightened me that there do exist good and evil in this world, while the mission of artists is to discover beauty from both of them. I believe we computer scientists and engineers should make use of Big Data in the same way, to reveal humanity and bring hope to this world. From my perspective, software systems, or more specifically, large-scale and data-intensive applications, is the Eighth Art besides the seven forms of art defined by Ricciotto Canudo and Hegel. Moreover, it has an advantage over all the other seven, which is the potential to connect every single person in this world, thus it could bring opportunity and equality to everyone.

With such a faith, I accomplished two projects in my senior year that I'm most proud of. The first project is the Salary Prediction Model developed during my internship at MPOWER Financing. With access to huge datasets, I built a Machine Learning model to predict students' potential salary based on their major, school, degree level, and home country. This model is then used as a credit reference by the company to give education loans to international students in order to help them reach academic potentials and achieve career goals. Although based on previous data, I believe that the Salary Prediction Model does not mean to define a path to wealth. Instead, it aims to discover more individuals with humble origins to attain remarkable success, and more students from ordinary schools to make extensive contributions. The second project is AuraMaze, which aims to enable art museum visitors to identify a painting with phone camera and learn everything about its background. Our apps have been published on App Store and Google Play, with a high identification accuracy covering more than 140,000 public domain paintings. We are also actively cooperating with museums and art institutes, such as University of Michigan Museum of Art and LSA College of History of Art, to promote our products to more users. I hope that the AuraMaze project not only provides art lovers with an accessible channel to art information, but also encourages more people to go to art museums and have face-to-face communication with artworks.

At present, I'm working on an even more exciting research project named Table Embeddings under instruction of Prof. Michael Cafarella. It's based on 233 million tables from Web HTML, from which we could mine unlimited treasure. In the future, there are more Big Data related projects awaiting for me to explore, either by building scalable and reliable systems to provide users with infrastructure of data communication, or by data mining that could enable us to develop more powerful and more intelligent applications. Anyway, we need to be cautious about the power of Big Data, which is just like Professor X's Cerebro, with which he has witnessed so much good and evil, but can still have hope for human beings. I agree with Martin Kleppmann's conclusion in Designing Data-Intensive Applications: "As software and data are having such a large impact on the world, we engineers must remember that we carry a responsibility to work toward the kind of world that we want to live in: a world that treats people with humanity and respect." The future of Big Data is challenging, but I'm ready to go on my adventure.
